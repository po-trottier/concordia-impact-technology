Food waste: a lesson from the past

As you probably know, food waste is basically non-existent in today’s society, but it wasn’t always like this. Today, people in developed countries all around the globe are very conscious of their wastefulness and try to minimize it everywhere they can. From farms to individuals’ fridges, passing by the supermarkets, almost no food is wasted and there are laws in place to make sure that it remains this way for the foreseeable future.

At home, our fridges notify us whenever something is about to go bad so that we can use it promptly. They even propose some possible recipes to cook the food that has the least amount of time left to be fresh. It is also generally badly seen to throw out food, but you should already know that. We all know it is best to bring our leftover food to the food banks and kitchen soups before it expires. On the other hand, large scale organizations (e.g. supermarket) have standards to respect to make sure that almost all their food is used properly. They have to send the food that’s about to go bad directly to non-profit organizations in their area. Large organizations and brands supporting the no food waste movement are almost everywhere and most of the population in Canada doesn’t even consider buying food that doesn’t have the 0FOODWASTE seal of approval.

For those who don’t know where the 0FOODWASTE program comes from, we have to go all the way back to December 2019. There are no official versions of this story since all written knowledge was lost in the mid-2000s but the origin story was passed from generation to generation in my family. Because my father and his mother were both historians, these kinds of special events that don’t mean much to the majority of people are seen as quite special. Anyway, let’s get back to the story. The end of 2019 was approaching rapidly and the environmental plaguing the planet was getting worse by the hours. The government of Canada decided it was time to act and they put in place a plan to help save the planet.

The first step was to survey all food corporations and brands in the country and classify them by their waste levels. They hoped that their evaluations would push citizens to buy from more eco-responsible brands. After the data was collected, all brands that threw away less than 5% of the weighted amount of food they produced or sold were awarded a 0FOODWASTE stamp. This was a clear invitation to food producers and distributors to stop wasting food for no reason. On top of that, if the company sent at least 50% of their almost-waste food to not-for-profit organizations they were awarded a gold stamp. The government put in place this constraint in hopes that it would help feed the poor and put to good use all this extra food. Finally, if the companies bought or sold at least 50% of their products locally they were awarded the diamond stamp. This last constraint aimed at reducing the carbon footprint of the food industry by diminishing the distance travelled by the food it served.

I think it is important to note that the companies that didn’t hop on board with the project at the time, no matter their size are basically all inexistent today. A good example of this is Walmart. They were the biggest multinational in the world with over $650 billion of revenue and almost 12 000 stores worldwide, and today they’re on the verge of going bankrupt.

In 2019, about 4.82 million tonnes of food were thrown out by supermarkets and food producers. At the time, this much food was worth nearly $21 billion. Most of this waste was because food would go past their “best before” labels, at which point they could not be sold anymore. On top of these millions of tonnes of food wasted during the production and distribution of the foodstuffs, another 2.38 million tonnes of food (worth more than $10 billion at the time) were wasted by individuals after they purchased the food. Combined, these quantities add up to around 58% of all the food produced in 2019. Needles to say these enormous amounts of food thrown away pushed people to start thinking about a solution to the problem. Many started asking grocery chains to give away their almost-bad food to non-profits around them.

As the movement gained traction, it attracted the Canadian government’s attention and it’s at that point that the idea of 0FOODWASTE started emerging. There were a few technical problems at the time though; The government needed a way to safely and accurately determine if food that was passed its “best-before” date was still safe to eat and safe to distribute to the public. To solve that problem, they sent an open invitation to any canadian companies that would want to try and develop a device that could accurately determine the amount of life left in a food. They had already seen a few prototypes in circulation but wanted a more secure and accurate way to measure the life-expectancy of food6.
